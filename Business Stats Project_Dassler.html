Problem Statement:¶

An online news portal wants to understand whether a new landing be more effective at drawing new subscribers than the old landing page.

The portal also wants a detailed analysis:

  1. Exploratory Data Analysis.
  2. Do the users spend more time on the new landing page than the old landing page?
  3. Is the conversion rate (the proportion of users who visit the landing page and get converted) for the new page greater than the conversion rate for the old page?
  4. Does the converted status depend on the preferred language?
  5. Is the mean time spent on the new page same for the different language users?

2. Do the users spend more time on the new landing page than the old landing page?¶

Ho: mean time spent on new landing page equals mean time spent on old landing page

Ha: mean time spent on new landing page does not equal mean time spent on old landing page

In [22]:
sample_mean_old=4.53
sample_std_old=2.58
sample_mean_new=6.22
sample_std_new=1.82

We are using a two-sample t-test:

  1. Because there are two sample means (old page and new page time spent on each page)
  2. The population standard deviation is unknown
  3. The time variable is a continuous variable
  4. We can assume a normal distribution for both treatment groups because the number of observations is greater than 30 in both groups.
In [23]:
compare_pages=ttest_ind(df_new["time_spent_on_the_page"],df_old["time_spent_on_the_page"],alternative="greater")
compare_pages
Out[23]:
Ttest_indResult(statistic=3.7867702694199856, pvalue=0.0001316123528095005)

As the p-value 0.00013 is less than the level of significance a=0.05, we can reject the null hypothesis and conclude with 95% confidence that the mean time spent on the new page is greater than the mean time spent on the old page.

3. Is the conversion rate (the proportion of users who visit the landing page and get converted) for the new page greater than the conversion rate for the old page?¶

Ho: The proportion of converted readers is the same on the new page as it is on the old page

Ha: The proportion of converted readers is greater for the new page than it is on the old page

Because both sample sizes are greater than 30, we can assume normal distributions for both the new page and old page proportion of converted readers. Both the mean (np) and n(1-p) are greater than 10, based on the countplots of converts above, further substantiating that a normal distribution can be applied to the binomial distribution.

The two samples are independent of each other because one reader's landing page treatment does not impact another reader's landing page treatment. They are random samples as well.

For the above reasons, a two-proportion z-test can be used to compare the two sample proportions of converted readers.

The p-value of the chi-squared test for independence between the language preferred and the conversion rate is 0.213, which is greater than the required a=.05 requirement. Therefore, we fail to reject the null hypothesis at the 95% confidence level, concluding that whether a reader was converted to a subscriber is independent of the reader's preferred language. Therefore, E-news Express knows with 95% confidence that whether the landing page is in English, French, or Spanish does not impact whether a reader is converted to a subscriber.

5. Is the mean time spent on the new page same for the different language users?¶

Because we are testing multiple sample means from independent populations (English, French, and Spanish speakers' preferred language does not depend on each other), an ANOVA test is most appropriate.

We can assume the mean time spent on the new page is normally distributed because the number of smaple observations is greater than 30.

However, we must first conduct a Levene test to understand if variances are equal or not.

Levene:

Ho:All variances between the time spent on the new pages are equal across all pages. Ha:At least one variance between the time spent on the new pages are not equal across all pages.

In [30]:
from scipy.stats import levene

statistic,p_value=levene(df_new["time_spent_on_the_page"][df_new["language_preferred"]=="English"],
                         df_new["time_spent_on_the_page"][df_new["language_preferred"]=="French"],
                         df_new["time_spent_on_the_page"][df_new["language_preferred"]=="Spanish"])
print(p_value)

0.46711357711340173

Because the p-value of the Levene test is greater than the significance level of a=.05, we fail to reject the null hypothesis and conclude with 95% confidence that variances between the time spent on the new pages are equal across new English, Spanish, and French pages.

We can now proceed with the ANOVA test:

Ho: mean time spent on new English page=mean time mean time spent on new French page=mean time spent on new Spanish page

Ha: at least one mean time spent on the new page is different between the English, French, and Spanish pages

In [31]:
test_stat,p_value=f_oneway(df_new["time_spent_on_the_page"][df_new["language_preferred"]=="English"],
                         df_new["time_spent_on_the_page"][df_new["language_preferred"]=="French"],
                         df_new["time_spent_on_the_page"][df_new["language_preferred"]=="Spanish"])
print(p_value)

0.43204138694325955

Because the p-value of the ANOVA test is greater than the significance level of a=.05, we fail to reject the null hypothesis and conclude with 95% confidence that mean time spent on the new pages are equal across new English, Spanish, and French pages.
